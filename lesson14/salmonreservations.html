Summary Of River Classifications

The grade of a river may (and usually does) change with the level of flow. Often a river or rapid will be given a numerical grade, and then a plus (+) or minus (-) to indicate if it is in the higher or lower end of the difficulty level.

Class I – Easy.Very small rough areas, few obstructions. Risk to swimmers is slight; self-rescue is easy (skill level: none).

Class II – Novice. Some rough water. Occasional maneuvering may be required. Medium-sized waves are easily avoided by trained paddlers (skill level: basic paddling).

Class III – Intermediate. Medium waves or strainers may be present but not much considerable danger. Complex maneuvering are often required (skill level: experienced paddling).

Class IV – Advanced. Large waves, long rapids, rocks, maybe a considerable drop, sharp maneuvers may be needed. Rapids may require "must make" moves above dangerous hazards (skill level: advanced whitewater experience).

Class V – Expert. Extremely long obstructed rapids, failure to execute a specific maneuver at a specific point may result in serious injury or death. Proper equipment, extensive experience, and practiced rescue skills are essential (skill level: expert).

Class VI– Extreme and Exploratory Rapids hat are not passable and any attempt to do so would result in serious injury, near drowning, or death. The consequences of errors are severe and rescue may be impossible.
